Doing The Next Best Thing

I think doing the next best thing is an important virtue to live by. Sometimes I don’t want to do anything. Like today. I told myself I was going to put my website online. I edited a few lines of HTML and then gave up. I’m not sure why I got so easily discouraged—it probably had a lot to do with the fact that I read a lot of other people’s crap online that made be less confident in what I am doing. Like this Silicon Valley bigwig who wrote, “If you’re not the best programmer in the world and are not doing something that doesn’t make you happy, you probably aren’t going to do your best!” I’m probably misquoting him, but it made me feel sad. I’m not sure if this is because I have high expectations of myself (high expectations aren’t a terrible thing to have, but they can oftentimes cause stress and other stupid stuff), or because he has high expectations of everyone else. All I know is, I reacted to what he and the other part of the Internet said, and it took away from what I wanted to do.

So this is where doing the next best thing comes in. It’s genius because it doesn’t make me feel lame or berated for absolutely sucking at something one day. Everybody must have days where they stink at doing things. That’s what the word Sunday was invited for. In fact, going to church was just a fancy socio-religious construct to allow people to have one day a week to do absolutely nothing. This probably isn’t true, but it makes me feel better, because there’s tons of atheists who need a day off, and just because they don’t go to church doesn’t mean they can’t exercise that right.

Doing the next best thing also puts the emphasis on the future. Economists love the theory of sunk cost—even if you snorkeled in the pond full of goldfish waste for 40 years, you can still always swim to shore and start making a profit off of highly-mercurial goldfish compost. In short, it doesn’t matter what crap you did in the past to bring you to your future success—there’s always time to discard and try anew, and eventually that process will yield happiness and success.

I’m trying to practice this experiment in real time. Let’s analyze what I did today: a symphony of nose-picking, an afternoon of football, and an hour conversation with my best friend from college. Not a bad day in the leisure department. But I also did a lot of procrastinating, especially in the evening when I wanted to actually accomplish stuff. But you know what? I freaking loved today. I didn’t attend church this morning, and I still managed to pump out a full day of religiously-authorized donothingness. And when I felt that I had to emerge from the goldfish poop-filled vacuum of my life and write, I listened to the economists and put fist to keyboard. And at 1:26 AM, it yielded something productive and enjoyable.
